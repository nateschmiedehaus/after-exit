# IRL Magazine Supply Chain and Distribution

## Purpose

This document answers four physical-world questions:

1. How should `After Exit` actually make a print magazine?
2. How should it get that object into the right hands?
3. When does paid subscription delivery make sense versus controlled circulation?
4. What does physical distribution mean to advertisers?

## Core View

The print product should not be treated like a mass-magazine newsstand play.

It should be treated like a `premium controlled-circulation and member-delivered object` with a mix of:

- paid subscribers
- charter members
- targeted direct delivery
- event distribution
- partner placement
- stockist presence in places where print already has cultural value

## Why Print Belongs

The logic is not nostalgia. It is function.

Print gives the brand:

- a durable object
- higher perceived value
- a better environment for photography and issue packaging
- a strong sponsor canvas
- a giftable and displayable artifact
- better fit for clubs, lounges, hotels, offices, and private rooms

Kinfolk says its quarterly magazine is sold in `100+ countries`, with print subscriptions delivered to readers' doors and the brand distributed through bookstores and newsagents globally.

Sources:

- [Kinfolk About](https://www.kinfolk.com/about)
- [Kinfolk Shop / Subscribe](https://www.kinfolk.com/shop/)

Monocle explicitly runs print subscriptions, back issues, special editions, film, retail, and a curated flagship-store and stockist program.

Sources:

- [Monocle Print](https://monocle.com/shop/print/)
- [Monocle Flagship Stores](https://app.api.monocle.com/about/flagship-stores/)

## Recommended Print Model

### Format

Recommended starting point:

- `quarterly`
- premium perfect-bound or softcover journal format
- substantial photography
- issue-led structure rather than fast-cycle news

Why quarterly:

- USPS Periodicals requires regular publication at least `4` times a year if the publication wants Periodicals mailing privileges.
- Quarterly fits the issue-based, image-led, slower premium positioning better than monthly.

Source:

- [USPS Periodicals overview](https://postalpro.usps.com/periodicals)

### Print object recommendation

Inference:

The object should feel closer to Kinfolk, Monocle specials, or a collectible journal than to a disposable newsstand monthly.

That means:

- strong cover stock
- matte or uncoated interior stock for photography and tactility
- restrained ad load
- enough trim and page count to feel worth keeping

## Physical Supply Chain

### 1. Editorial close

The issue needs:

- editorial close date
- final ad close date
- image-rights clearance
- contributor agreements
- sponsor approvals only for sponsored materials, never editorial

### 2. Design and prepress

Workflow:

1. final layouts
2. image correction and proofing
3. ad-material intake
4. printer-ready PDF generation
5. hard or soft proof approval

Monocle's print operations explicitly reference print specifications and ICC profiles for advertising materials.

Source:

- [Monocle contacts / print specs reference](https://monocle.com/about/contacts/the-monocle-shop-tokyo/)

### 3. Printer selection

Select printers based on:

- quality of color reproduction
- premium paper options
- perfect binding / saddle / specialty finish capability
- mailing and polybag / wrap options
- warehouse and fulfillment integration
- reprint flexibility
- domestic and international distribution support

Recommended process:

- quote at least `3` printers
- compare quality, not only cost
- ask for sample books and magazines
- test one pilot run before locking annual volume

### 4. Mailing and fulfillment

There are two likely phases.

#### Phase A: early issues

Use standard fulfillment and parcel / flat mailing before optimizing for Periodicals.

Why:

- lower administrative burden at small scale
- more flexibility while issue frequency, counts, and targeting are still changing

#### Phase B: stable cadence

Pursue USPS Periodicals only once cadence and list hygiene are stable.

USPS says Periodicals are for publications whose primary purpose is transmitting information to an established list of subscribers or requesters, published at regular intervals at least four times a year, with record-keeping and circulation standards.

Sources:

- [USPS Periodicals](https://postalpro.usps.com/periodicals)
- [USPS DMM 207 Periodicals](https://pe.usps.com/cpim/ftp/manuals/dmm300/207.pdf)
- [USPS Applying for Periodicals Mailing Privileges](https://about.usps.com/handbooks/dm204/eligibility.html)

## Distribution Architecture

The right physical distribution model is mixed.

### 1. Paid subscriber delivery

Copies mailed directly to:

- Reader members
- Circle members
- Patrons
- gift recipients

Use this for:

- renewal logic
- predictable delivery
- strongest proof of paid demand

### 2. Charter and member gifting

Use the issue as a social object:

- member gifts to peers
- welcome kits
- speaker or contributor copies
- spouse / partner copies for certain programs

This is not only circulation. It is referral infrastructure.

### 3. Event distribution

Distribute copies at:

- city salons
- dinners
- retreats
- partner briefings
- private-bank or family-governance events

This is especially useful because the issue can act as:

- invitation artifact
- recap artifact
- sponsor proof
- reason to follow up

### 4. Controlled public-place distribution

Alliance for Audited Media provides verified circulation categories that matter here.

Two especially relevant categories:

- `Verified individual use`
  Copies sent to named individuals with an affinity for the publication.
- `Verified public place`
  Copies placed in locations such as waiting rooms, hotel rooms, airline clubs, restaurants, and similar public places.

Sources:

- [AAM Verified Individual Use](https://support.auditedmedia.com/verified-individual-use)
- [AAM Verified Public Place](https://support.auditedmedia.com/verified-public-place)
- [AAM Media Audits](https://auditedmedia.com/media-audits)

This means `After Exit` can eventually place copies in:

- private-bank lounges
- family-office offices
- members' clubs
- boutique hotels
- executive health clinics
- airport lounges and airline clubs

without pretending those copies are the same thing as paid subscribers.

### 5. Curated stockist program

Do not pursue generic national newsstand distribution early.

Do pursue:

- design-forward bookstores
- airport bookstores in target corridors
- hotel boutiques
- club retail
- founder-city concept stores

Monocle's flagship-store program is a useful pattern: trusted partners in places where print matters.

Source:

- [Monocle Flagship Stores](https://app.api.monocle.com/about/flagship-stores/)

## Inventory and Circulation Logic

The print run should not be one undifferentiated pile.

Break it into buckets:

1. paid subscribers
2. member and patron copies
3. partner and sponsor copies
4. event and salon inventory
5. controlled public-place placements
6. stockist copies
7. archive and press copies

This matters because advertisers will care about:

- how many copies were paid
- how many went to named individuals
- how many reached target venues
- how many were distributed at curated events

## Advertiser Value of IRL Distribution

For advertisers, physical distribution is not just `impressions`.

It can mean:

- time spent with a premium object
- physical presence in homes and offices
- pass-along reading in clubs, lounges, and waiting areas
- contextual adjacency to trust-rich environments
- direct penetration into named high-value accounts or venues

### 1. Trust and engagement

MPA says magazine media carries high consumer trust and strong engagement, and that consumers value the touch and feel of print.

Source:

- [MPA Magazine Media Factbook release](https://magazine.org/mpa-the-association-of-magazine-media-releases-its-2021-mpa-magazine-media-factbook/)

### 2. Audited circulation matters

AAM exists specifically to give advertisers trustworthy, verified audience data and transparent reporting.

Source:

- [AAM Who We Are](https://auditedmedia.com/about/who-we-are)

### 3. Controlled circulation can be premium

For a brand like `After Exit`, controlled circulation is not a weakness if it is:

- targeted
- auditable
- high-affinity
- placed in the right rooms

For example, verified public place copies in hotels, airline clubs, medical or executive spaces, and other premium locations can be valuable if they match the advertiser's target account map.

Sources:

- [AAM Verified Public Place](https://support.auditedmedia.com/verified-public-place)
- [AAM U.S. Rules Book 2025](https://auditedmedia.com/hubfs/AAM-US-Bylaws-and-Rules.pdf)

## What "Getting In People's Hands" Means Commercially

For advertisers, this should be sold as `qualified physical presence`, not generic print scale.

That can include:

- copies mailed to named principals
- copies placed in target clubs, hotels, and lounges
- copies handed out at private dinners and salons
- copies included in founding-member or partner gift boxes
- copies delivered into high-value offices and family-capital environments

The value proposition is:

- fewer copies
- better hands
- better rooms
- better context

## Advertiser Packaging Recommendations

### 1. Issue partner

Best for:

- private banks
- wealth platforms
- family governance firms
- longevity brands
- luxury travel groups

Package can include:

- premium print placement
- digital issue support
- event alignment
- controlled circulation to target venues or target people

### Ad load guidance

Recommendation:

Treat the print object as a `low-ad-load environment`.

Inference:

For a premium niche title like `After Exit`, the issue should feel editorially dominant and commercially selective. In practice that means:

- keep total ad load visibly below mainstream consumer-magazine clutter
- reserve only a small number of premium positions
- make scarcity part of the rate logic
- avoid running so many ads that the issue stops feeling collectible

The point is not just aesthetics. It is commercial positioning.

Low-ad-load magazines can justify higher rates because advertisers are buying:

- a quieter environment
- stronger adjacency
- higher perceived prestige
- more memorable placement
- less competition from other brands in the same issue

MPA argues that magazine media benefits from high consumer trust, strong engagement, and the tactile value of print, all of which support premium advertiser context.

Source:

- [MPA Magazine Media Factbook release](https://magazine.org/mpa-the-association-of-magazine-media-releases-its-2021-mpa-magazine-media-factbook/)

### Suggested launch ad-load rule

Inference:

- target `10%` to `15%` of total pages as paid advertising at launch
- treat `20%` as a ceiling, not a target
- protect the front of book, major photo essays, and closing sequences from clutter

This should be presented as a brand rule, not just a production choice.

### What advertisers are really buying in a low-ad-load issue

- category exclusivity or limited adjacency
- fewer competing messages
- stronger recall because the issue is not crowded
- association with a collectible object
- a premium environment that signals quality before the ad is even seen

### 2. Venue or venue-network package

Example:

- private-bank lounges
- executive clinics
- members' clubs
- boutique hotel groups

Sell as:

- issue placement in verified public places
- on-site events or salons
- associated digital recap or note

### 3. Target-account distribution package

Highly selective delivery of copies to:

- a sponsor's top accounts
- a sponsor's priority cities
- host and partner venues

This is especially meaningful for B2B or advisory advertisers.

### 4. Event-plus-print package

Combine:

- salon sponsorship
- issue distribution
- post-event recap
- targeted follow-up copies

This is stronger than an isolated ad page.

### 5. Bespoke editorial-adjacent package

For the right partner only.

Examples:

- a labeled sponsor-backed research dossier
- a city-guide alignment
- a family-governance or longevity briefing package
- a printed insert or discrete supplement with tight design control

Rule:

These should feel like custom publishing inside a premium media environment, not like advertorial sludge.

## Commercial Packaging Rules

### 1. Sell scarcity, not volume

The pitch should be:

- fewer copies
- better hands
- fewer ad pages
- better adjacency
- more integration across print, rooms, and follow-up

### 2. Package print with distribution intelligence

Advertisers should be told not only that their ad ran, but:

- how many copies went to paid subscribers
- how many went to members
- how many reached verified public-place venues
- which cities and venue types were hit
- which events were tied to that issue

This is where AAM-style verified categories become commercially useful.

### 3. Package print with rooms

For this brand, a print ad page by itself is rarely the most valuable product.

Better package shapes:

- issue + salon
- issue + target-account distribution
- issue + private briefing
- issue + retreat or dinner placement

### 4. Protect adjacency

Avoid placing direct competitors too close together.

The quieter and more selective the issue feels, the more rate integrity you can maintain.

### 5. Protect the editorial spine

Never let sponsor load collapse the central magazine argument:

- strong reporting
- strong imagery
- restrained commercial presence
- clear labeling

If the issue looks over-monetized, both readers and advertisers lose.

## Metrics That Matter

For the print business:

- total print run
- paid subscriber copies
- verified individual-use copies
- verified public-place copies
- event-distributed copies
- stockist sell-through
- delivery success rate
- claim / replacement rate

For advertisers:

- number of named high-value recipients
- number of qualified venues reached
- event attendance tied to the issue
- partner follow-up conversations
- QR / URL / code redemptions from print

## Recommended Path

### Stage 1

- quarterly issue
- paid and member delivery only
- salon and retreat hand-distribution
- no broad newsstand strategy

### Stage 2

- curated stockists in launch cities
- partner-office and club placements
- formal advertiser distribution reporting

### Stage 3

- verified circulation categories through AAM
- deeper hotel, lounge, clinic, and club programs
- target-account print programs for advertisers

## Bottom Line

The right IRL strategy is not `print as mass circulation`.

It is `print as premium controlled distribution`.

That makes the magazine more useful to readers, more giftable inside trusted networks, and more valuable to advertisers because every copy can be placed in the right hands, rooms, and cities.
